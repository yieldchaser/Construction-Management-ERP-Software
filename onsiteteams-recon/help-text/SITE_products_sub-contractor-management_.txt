Industry Challenges Why Subcontractor Control Must Be Digital Subcontractors handle 40-60% of project execution, yet most companies track their work through verbal commitments and Excel sheets. Without structured control systems, overbilling, retention loss, and payment disputes become inevitable. Informal Work Orders Without digital work orders, subcontractor engagements start with verbal agreements or informal documents. Scope definitions stay vague. Quantities, rates, and timelines remain undocumented. When billing begins, disputes arise over what was agreed versus what gets claimed. Scope creep happens silently as additional work gets added without approvals or budget revisions. Unverified Progress Billing Manual progress tracking cannot validate executed quantities against assigned work. Subcontractors submit bills based on time elapsed rather than measurable completion. Approval teams lack visibility into actual site progress. Bills get processed without quantity verification, leading to payments that exceed completed work by 10-20% across projects. Retention Tracking Failures Spreadsheet-based retention management results in inconsistent deductions and missed releases. Retention percentages get calculated manually with frequent errors. Release schedules disappear in email chains. When projects close, recovering retention becomes contentious because documentation is incomplete or disputed, causing cashflow strain and relationship damage. No Performance Visibility Without centralized tracking, you cannot identify which subcontractors deliver quality work on time and which ones consistently overbill or delay. Performance data remains anecdotal and scattered across project teams. Future subcontractor selection relies on memory and relationships rather than execution records, productivity metrics, and billing accuracy data.
